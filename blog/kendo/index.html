Kendo

Dec 11th, 2014 6:15 am

My journey to learn the Katana continued last night. I found a Kendo (Sword Way) class in Palo Alto, and talked with the instructors as well as watching for a while. I will not be taking this class because:

  • It doesn’t really teach Kendo
  • It doesn’t teach effectively
  • It is at a horrible time for me

What? A Kendo class that doesn’t teach The Way of the Sword? How can that be?

Well, you need to understand a bit about the Japanese culture and history to understand why Kendo is not really Kendo.

The Japanese are an amazing people: they are incredibly disciplined with both body and mind. This makes them very powerful. They do amazing things both individually and in groups. The Japanese are also very obedient. They follow orders to the letter. They obey their masters.

Where these traits came from, I do not know, but together they are scary. If you read Artemis Fowl, read/watch Helsing, or any number of other books and movies, you may have noticed that the most scary person in these are the guardians of a “master”. When you are guarding your master you have exactly two rules:

  • I must protect my master
  • I must obey my master

Two rules. Only two. “I must be nice to pigeons!” Not a rule. “I must not run over babies with my Hummer!” Not a rule. When Asimov wrote the three laws of robotics, he added a third rule about self preservation, but that one is actually redundant with the first rule as long as you are dealing with a single master. Basically Asimov agrees with my rules (or caused me to recognize my rules given I love Asimov’s work).

In the small, scary guardians are fine. Even Artemis tended not to abuse the world too much with his Butler. But when you have a highly disciplined society that obeys these two rules… bad things are going to happen. Like WW2.

So Kendo was normal going into WW2: It was a way to learn how to wield a Katana. I suspect the history of its development went like this:

  • To practice we will use our Katanas! [Much mayhem]
  • To practice we will use an Iaito [Unsharpened Katana]! [Much mayhem]
  • To practice we will use a Bokken [Wooden Unsharpened Sword]! [Still some mayhem]
  • To practice we will use a Shinai [Bamboo Sword]! Ah! That works and keeps people in one piece.

So Kendo progressed to the Shinai as a weapon, but otherwise was exactly the same. To wield a Katana you slice your opponent, so to practice with a Shinai you make exactly the same motion. That was Kendo with a Shinai.

Until WW2.

After WW2 Japan was rebuilt both physically and mentally. I can’t say how a group of people managed to do this to themselves, but when they disarmed militarily, they similarly disarmed (boxed) themselves mentally too. It is among the more amazing cultural transformations I can imagine. Almost like Divergent.

And within the box, you can no longer practice Kendo. You can’t practice wielding a Katana if you know you are supposed to be disarmed. That is dishonest to your principles.

But the Japanese also love traditions. So they changed Kendo into being about ‘tapping’. You could call it ‘hitting’, but it really isn’t. And in either case, it has nothing to do with wielding a Katana. It would actually mess you up.

So very interesting. Kendo isn’t Kendo.

There was a period where pre-WW2 Kendo people were still around, and still did Kendo the right way. They were called ‘Heavy Hitters’, but that is a misnomer. They were slicing you with a Shinai, not hitting you at all. That is the way of the sword. Still looking for one of them to teach me…
